## RESEARCH AREA 1: WASHINGTON STATE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 School District Budget Adoption Requirements (RCW 28A.505)

Washington State law establishes a comprehensive framework for school district budgeting through RCW Chapter 28A.505 and WAC Chapter 392-123.

**Budget Preparation and Timeline:**
- Districts must prepare budgets by July 10 of each year (RCW 28A.505.040)
- Budgets must include a four-year plan with enrollment projections
- Public notices must be published at least twice over two consecutive weeks (RCW 28A.505.050)
- First-class districts must hold public hearings and adopt budgets by August 31 (WAC 392-123-054)
- Second-class districts must adopt by August 1
- Adopted budgets filed with educational service districts by September 3

**Four-Year Planning Requirement:**
RCW 28A.505.040 mandates inclusion of enrollment projections and estimates for maintaining program levels and supplemental contract obligations in multi-year planning documents. This is significant because it means the district is required to produce forward-looking enrollment projections as part of the budget adoption process.

**Budget Format:**
RCW 28A.505.090 requires budgets to be prepared in the format prescribed by OSPI, consistent with the Accounting Manual for Public School Districts. Budgets in non-prescribed formats are "not official and will have no legal effect."

**Sources:**
- [RCW 28A.505 Full Text](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=28A.505&full=true)
- [WAC 392-123 Full Chapter](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=392-123&full=true)
- [RCW 28A.505.060 - Hearing and Adoption](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=28A.505.060)

### 1.2 Balanced Budget Requirements

**Core Requirement (RCW 28A.505.130):**
Estimated expenditures CANNOT exceed estimated revenues plus beginning fund balance minus ending reserve balance. Any budget adopted without written OSPI permission that contains expenditures exceeding available resources is "null and void and shall not be considered an appropriation."

**Receivables from Future Years (RCW 28A.505.110):**
Districts may include receivables from future fiscal periods ONLY with written permission from the superintendent of public instruction, which comes with binding conditions.

**WAC 392-123-053 Compliance:**
Budgets lacking written superintendent permission for revenue accruals or containing deficits are "null and void."

**CRITICAL FOR INVESTIGATION:** If Kent School District adopted budgets based on enrollment projections they knew or should have known were overstated, those budgets may have violated the balanced budget requirement by overstating anticipated revenues.

**Sources:**
- [RCW 28A.505.130](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=28A.505&full=true)
- [RCW 28A.505.110](https://law.justia.com/codes/washington/2022/title-28a/chapter-28a-505/section-28a-505-110/)

### 1.3 Budget Amendments and Extensions

**First-Class Districts (RCW 28A.505.170 / WAC 392-123-071):**
- Emergency appropriations (fire, flood, earthquake) can be made by majority board resolution without notice or hearing
- Non-emergency extensions require public notice, majority board vote, and filing with educational service districts by August 31

**Second-Class Districts (RCW 28A.505.180 / WAC 392-123-072):**
Must petition the superintendent through their educational service district, presenting documented justification and the latest budget status report.

**KEY FINDING:** Washington law does not specify a particular percentage threshold that triggers a mandatory budget amendment. However, WAC 392-123-115 requires monthly budget status reports with "a brief written explanation of any significant deviations in revenue and/or expenditure projections that may affect the financial status of the district." This creates an affirmative duty to disclose material variances.

**Sources:**
- [RCW 28A.505.170](https://app.leg.wa.gov/RCW/default.aspx?cite=28A.505.170)
- [WAC 392-123-115](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=392-123-115)

### 1.4 Monthly Financial Reporting Requirements

**WAC 392-123-115 -- Monthly Budget Status Reports:**
- Districts MUST prepare monthly budget status reports for each fund
- Reports MUST be provided to each board member at the regular monthly meeting
- Administration MUST provide "a brief written explanation of any significant deviations in revenue and/or expenditure projections"
- OSPI can require additional monthly reporting with written notice

**CRITICAL FOR INVESTIGATION:** If Kent SD's administration failed to provide adequate written explanations of significant enrollment deviations in monthly budget status reports, this would constitute a violation of WAC 392-123-115.

**Sources:**
- [WAC 392-123-115](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=392-123-115)
- [F-198 Budget Status Report Form](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/sites/default/files/2025-02/f-198_budget_status_report.pdf)

### 1.5 OSPI Oversight Authority

**Superintendent Review (RCW 28A.505.140):**
- Must adopt rules ensuring proper budgetary procedures and monthly financial statements
- Must provide written notice if budgets fail to comply
- Districts must submit revised budgets within 30 days of noncompliance notice

**Fund Withholding (RCW 28A.505.120 / WAC 392-123-065):**
- OSPI may withhold state funds upon noncompliance with binding restrictions
- Written notice with specific reasons required before withholding

**Sources:**
- [RCW 28A.505.140](http://wa.elaws.us/rcw/28A.505.140)
- [OSPI Guidance and Tools](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment/guidance-and-tools)

### 1.6 Binding Conditions Framework

**What Triggers Binding Conditions (WAC 392-123-060):**
- A school district enters binding conditions when it is unable to produce a balanced budget
- The district must petition OSPI at least 20 days before budget adoption
- Petition must include a board resolution requesting permission to budget receivables collectible in future periods

**What Binding Conditions Include:**
- Fund balance targets (e.g., greater than negative $5,000,000 by specified dates)
- Revenue projection minimums (e.g., exceeding $35,000,000)
- Year-end fund balance thresholds (e.g., greater than $0)
- Monthly budget status reports to OSPI and ESD
- Quarterly review meetings with state and local education officials
- Review of expenditures, revenues, enrollment trends, staffing levels, and cash flow projections

**What Binding Conditions Do NOT Include:**
Closing schools, eliminating programs, reducing staff, or mandating labor contract renegotiation.

**Escalation Path:**
1. Binding Conditions (WAC 392-123-060) -- oversight and financial benchmarks
2. Financial Oversight Committee -- convened after two consecutive years or inability to develop viable plan
3. Enhanced Financial Oversight -- state takes "full control over all financial decisions"
4. Dissolution -- consolidation with neighboring districts

**Exemption (2024-2026):**
For fiscal years 2024-2025 and 2025-2026, districts with interfund loans exceeding their negative fund balance position are exempt from accepting binding conditions, provided the loan transaction date occurs by June 30, 2024.

**Currently Monitored Districts (as of 2025):**
- Mount Baker SD (binding conditions)
- Tukwila SD (binding conditions, January 31, 2025)
- Yelm SD (binding conditions, September 4, 2024)
- Shelton SD (binding conditions, January 10, 2025)
- Mabton SD (binding conditions, June 4, 2025)
- Bellevue SD (binding conditions, July 10, 2025)
- Prescott SD (enhanced financial oversight, facing dissolution)
- Marysville SD (financial oversight committee, $17.5M deficit)

### 1.7 Public Disclosure Requirements

**Public Records Act (RCW 42.56):**
- All public records maintained by school districts must be made available to the public
- School districts are self-governing public entities subject to PRA
- Financial documents must be retained per the Public Schools Records Retention Schedule

**Sources:**

### 1.8 Fiduciary Duty Standards for School Board Members

**Three Core Fiduciary Duties:**
1. **Duty of Care** -- Acting in good faith, with reasonable care, including actively reviewing financial documents, budgets, and policies
2. **Duty of Loyalty** -- Putting the organization's interests before personal interests
3. **Duty of Obedience** -- Ensuring the organization acts in accordance with its mission and applicable laws

**Financial Oversight Specifics:**
- Board directors must actively review financial documents, budgets, and policies
- Implement robust financial controls
- Conduct audits and monitor financial transactions
- Understand risks and make intentional decisions about acceptable risks
- Think short-term and long-term with depth of knowledge to understand financial statements

**Common Breaches:**
- Failing to attend meetings or stay informed
- Approving budgets or financial statements without proper review or understanding

**Sources:**
- [3 Legal Duties Every Board Member Must Follow](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/articles/nfp/2023/three-legal-duties-every-board-member-must-follow)
- [Fiduciary Responsibilities of Board Members](https://board-room.org/blog/fiduciary-responsibility-of-board-members/)

### 1.9 Washington State Auditor Requirements

**Audit Frequency and Scope:**
- SAO must examine financial affairs of all local governments at least once every three years
- All local governments must submit annual financial reports within 150 days of fiscal year end (RCW 43.09.230)
- Accountability audits evaluate compliance with state laws, regulations, and district policies
- School Programs Account funds audits specifically examining enrollment reporting, teacher education/experience, and student transportation reporting

**Federal Audit Requirements:**
Districts spending $750,000+ in federal awards annually must arrange for a federal compliance audit.

**CRITICAL FOR INVESTIGATION:** SAO audits specifically verify that districts are "properly reporting student enrollment" since "districts receiving a large portion of their funding based on this reporting." This creates an audit trail for enrollment reporting accuracy.

**Sources:**
- [About Local Government Audits - SAO](https://sao.wa.gov/about-audits/about-local-government-audits)
- [Areas SAO Examines in School Audits](https://sao.wa.gov/the-audit-connection-blog/2021/areas-sao-will-examine-upcoming-schools-and-esd-audits)

### 1.10 Revenue Recognition and Apportionment

**Enrollment-Based Funding:**
- Over 78% of school district General Fund revenue comes from the state
- Funding calculated using the prototypical school funding formula based on enrollment
- Districts report enrollment monthly on Form P-223 (September through June)
- Most funding based on Annual Average FTE (AAFTE)
- State distributes money monthly based on apportionment formulas

**True-Up Mechanism:**
- State bases initial funding on prior-year projections
- In January, OSPI "true[s] up the apportionment to actual average student enrollment"
- Enrollment revisions affect the following year's levy base and LAP funding
- Audit findings can result in allocation adjustments after the school year ends

**CRITICAL FOR INVESTIGATION:** The true-up mechanism means districts that overproject enrollment receive excess funding in the early months of the school year, which is then clawed back. This creates a financial incentive to overproject enrollment (even temporarily) and a financial shock when actual enrollment is reconciled.

**Sources:**
- [OSPI School Apportionment](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment)
- [OSPI Financial Reporting Summary](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment/school-publications/financial-reporting-summary)
- [Enrollment Reporting Guidance](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment/guidance-and-tools/enrollment-reporting)

### 1.11 Director Liability (RCW 28A.505.150)

**Civil Liability:**
Directors, officers, or employees who **knowingly or negligently** violate budget appropriation limits are held "civilly liable, jointly and severally, for such expenditures in excess of such appropriation(s), including consequential damages."

**Forfeiture of Office:**
If a violation is found to have been done **knowingly**, the director, officer, or employee "shall immediately forfeit his or her office or employment, and the judgment in any such action shall so provide."

**CRITICAL FOR INVESTIGATION:** This statute establishes both civil liability and automatic removal from office for knowing violations of budget appropriation limits. If enrollment projections were knowingly overstated to create artificially balanced budgets, this provision could be triggered.

**Sources:**
- [RCW 28A.505.150 Full Text](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=28A.505.150)
- [Justia RCW 28A.505.150](https://law.justia.com/codes/washington/2005/title28a/28a.505.150.html)

### 2.2 Budget Deficit History

**2015-2016 Enrollment Shortfall (CRITICAL PRECEDENT):**
- Enrollment projections were off by approximately 500 students
- Cost the district approximately $7 million in state funding
- Fund balance deteriorated from $14.1M to $3.8M to negative $6.9M over two years
- **Spending reductions did not begin until spring 2016**, months after fall 2015 enrollment revealed the problem
- Staff balanced by reducing 55 FTE positions, cutting M&O costs by 20%
- This event should have served as a warning about enrollment projection risks

**2023-2024 Budget Shortfall ($31.5 Million):**
- Projected deficit of at least $31.5M for 2024-2025 without action
- Primary cause: End of $99M in ESSER funding
- District included $31.5M of ESSER in the 2023-2024 budget
- Fund balance at approximately $60M (12.7% of budget)
- Board policy requires minimum 5% reserve (~$25M)
- Board voted 4-1 to authorize superintendent to develop cuts plan (Donald Cook dissented)

**2025-2026 Budget ($8M cuts growing to $10.5M):**
- Budget adopted in June 2025 assumed flat enrollment of 24,222 students
- October 1 count revealed only 23,430 students (shortfall of 792)
- Funding gap grew from $14.9M to $35.3M (increase of $20.4M)
- Breakdown: K-12 enrollment decline $8.2M + Inclusive Ed decline $6.2M + federal cuts $6.0M
- $12.4M covered from unassigned fund balance
- $5.2M cut from central office materials/supplies
- $1.9M saved from leaving central office positions vacant
- Remaining $3.4M in cuts still to be identified

**2026-2027 Projected Deficit ($30.5M to $35M):**
- Staff proposed $7.5M annual reductions for three consecutive years
- Nonrenewal notices prepared for up to 408 provisional teachers (1 in 5 certificated educators)
- Projected enrollment decline of 1,365 students over three years
- Finance Director Parungao warned: "the district cannot continue on a path of using the fund balance as a way to cover deficits as eventually that money will run out"

## RESEARCH AREA 3: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Washington State Enrollment Trends Post-Pandemic

**Statewide Data:**
- Washington K-12 enrollment dropped approximately 3-4% during/after the pandemic
- State experienced 9.2% enrollment growth from 2012 to 2020, then 4.2% loss during/after pandemic
- Enrollment projected to reach 1,033,500 by fall 2030 (down 59,300 or 5.4% from fall 2023)
- PreK-8: Projected decline from 739,800 to 707,700 (-4.3%)
- Grades 9-12: Projected decline from 353,000 to 325,800 (-7.7%)

**Alternative Education Growth:**
- Home schooling: 28,000+ students in 2022-23 (up 35% from 2019-20)
- Private school enrollment: Jumped 26% from ~65,000 to ~82,000

**Contributing Factors:**
- Declining birth rates (accelerated during pandemic)
- Migration patterns
- Shift to home schooling and private schools
- Charter school growth

**Sources:**
- [Seattle Times: Declining Public School Enrollment](https://www.seattletimes.com/seattle-news/data/declining-public-school-enrollment-heres-how-wa-compares/)
- [Columbia Basin Herald: Enrollment Decline](https://columbiabasinherald.com/news/2024/jun/18/public-school-enrollment-declining-in-wa-across-the-nation-as-spending-increases/)

### 3.2 Comparable District Analysis

**Kent School District (25,000+ students, King County):**
- Enrollment decline: ~8-9% from 2018 peak to 2024
- Budget response: Delayed spending reductions, relied on ESSER, then dramatic cuts
- Levy: Three failures before smaller measure passed

**Renton School District (~15,000 students, King County):**
- Down 8.3% (1,226 students) since 2020
- $8.2M reduction for 2023-24 through unfilled positions
- $11M+ additional reductions needed for 2024-25
- Proactively addressing ESSER cliff

**Tacoma Public Schools (~29,000 students, Pierce County):**
- $10M shortfall 2023-24, eliminated 22 administrative positions
- $40M shortfall 2024-25, eliminated 38 administrative positions
- Total reductions: $9.5M staffing + $20M expenditures
- Enrollment rebounded slightly to 29,010 but still below pre-pandemic 30,406

**Auburn School District (~16,000 students, King County):**
- Enrollment actually UP 2.4% (408 students) since 2019
- Partly due to new transitional kindergarten program

**Seattle Public Schools (~49,000 students, King County):**
- $105M deficit projected for 2024-2025
- Considered closing or consolidating dozens of schools
- Enrollment down ~280 students in 2024-25

**Bellevue School District (~20,000 students, King County):**
- Entered binding conditions July 10, 2025
- Spent $10M+ more per year than revenue since pandemic
- Reserves depleted below 5% minimum
- $20M in cuts for 2025-26

**COMPARATIVE OBSERVATION:** Kent's enrollment decline (~8-9%) is within the range experienced by similar districts (Renton at 8.3%), but Kent's budget response appears more delayed than Renton's or Tacoma's. Tacoma made $30M in cuts across two years while Kent relied on ESSER and fund balance draws before addressing structural deficits.

### 3.3 OSPI Enrollment Projection Methodology

**Required Reporting:**
- Districts report enrollment monthly on Form P-223 (September-June)
- Reporting uses headcount AND full-time equivalent (FTE)
- Most funding based on Annual Average FTE (AAFTE)
- OSPI provides Student Enrollment Cohort Projections (Report 1049)

**Standard Methods:**
The most common enrollment projection method nationally is the **cohort survival ratio**, which calculates the fraction of students in one grade who pass to the next grade, averaged across 5 years. This method works best in stable populations.

**Advanced Forecasting Methods:**
Best practice calls for incorporating:
- Births to district residents (K-to-birth ratios)
- Total population by age group
- Migration and housing development patterns
- Economic outlook
- Historical capture rates

**Kent's Specific Problem:**
Kent's October 2024 count showed enrollment was off by 284 students from a projection of essentially flat enrollment. However, "enrollment projections have generally been accurate within 150-200 students over recent years" according to district staff. The 792-student shortfall in 2025 was dramatically outside historical accuracy ranges.

**Sources:**
- [OSPI Enrollment Reporting](https://ospi.k12.wa.us/policy-funding/school-apportionment/guidance-and-tools/enrollment-reporting)
- [FLO Analytics: Enrollment Forecasting](https://flo-analytics.com/about/news-insights/student-enrollment-forecasting-explained-0/)
- [FLO Analytics: What Methods Work Best](https://www.flo-analytics.com/news/enrollment-projections-what-methods-work-best/)

### 3.4 GFOA/ASBO National Best Practices

**GFOA Best Practices in School Budgeting:**
- Strategic financial plan providing 3-5 year perspective
- Cost analysis and staffing analysis aligned to actual enrollment
- System to implement plan and monitor progress with adjustments
- Academic and finance collaboration

**Key Standard:** Budget projections should be based on realistic, data-driven enrollment estimates, with clear documentation of assumptions and methodology. Multi-year plans should incorporate sensitivity analysis showing the impact of enrollment variance scenarios.

## RESEARCH AREA 4: LEGAL PRECEDENTS

### 4.1 RCW 28A.505.150 -- Director Personal Liability

**The most directly applicable legal provision:**

Directors, officers, or employees who **knowingly or negligently** incur expenditures in excess of appropriations are:
- Civilly liable, jointly and severally, for excess expenditures plus consequential damages
- Subject to automatic forfeiture of office if violation found to be knowing

**Application to Kent SD:** If the district adopted budgets based on enrollment projections that were knowingly or negligently overstated, and actual spending exceeded what actual revenue could support, board members who approved those budgets could face personal liability under this statute.

### 4.7 McCleary v. Washington -- Constitutional Funding Duty

**Key Holding (2012):**
The Washington Supreme Court ruled that the state failed to meet its constitutional duty under Article IX ("paramount duty") to "make ample provision for the education of all children." The state was found in contempt and fined $100,000/day until compliance.

**Resolution (2017-2018):**
Legislature adopted the prototypical school funding model, pouring billions of new dollars into K-12 education. The model sets staffing standards for every school and adjusts funding based on enrollment.

**RELEVANCE TO KENT:** The McCleary decision established that education funding is a constitutional obligation. If a district's financial mismanagement through enrollment projection manipulation undermines the delivery of constitutionally required education, it raises questions beyond mere statutory compliance.

## RESEARCH AREA 5: FINANCIAL IMPACT CONTEXT

### 5.1 Washington Prototypical School Funding Formula

**Structure:**
The formula establishes minimum staffing levels and non-staff costs for prototypical schools:
- Elementary: 400 student FTE base
- Middle: 432 student FTE base
- High School: 600 student FTE base

**Staffing Allocations (per 400 elementary students):**
- 1.2 building administrators (principal/assistant principal)
- 0.6 librarians
- 0.49 guidance counselors
- 1.66 custodians
- Plus classified staff, nurses, social workers, etc.

**Materials, Supplies, and Operating Costs (MSOC):**
- Minimum allocation: $1,614.28 per FTE student (adjusted for inflation from 2026-27)
- Additional $214.84 per FTE for grades 9-12

**CRITICAL CONTEXT:** Under this formula, each student lost to enrollment decline translates directly to reduced staffing allocations and MSOC funding. For Kent's 792-student shortfall, the formula reduction would be approximately equivalent to 2 prototypical elementary schools worth of staffing.

### 5.4 OSPI Financial Health Indicators Model

**Model Structure (developed after HB 1431):**
| Indicator | Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Fund Balance to Revenue Ratio | 40% | Primary solvency indicator |
| Expenditures to Revenue Ratio | 35% | Structural balance indicator |
| Days Cash on Hand | 15% | Liquidity indicator |
| Four-Year Budget Summary Plan | 10% | Tracks years with negative fund balance |

**Scoring:**
- Points range from 0 to 4 in each category
- Combined score = sum of (indicator score x weight)
- Scores 0 to 1.75 = "Financial Warning" designation
- Perfect score = 4.0

**Statewide Trend:**
- 2020-21: Statewide average hit high of 3.6 (ESSER inflated)
- 2022-23: Declined to 2.85
- 19 of 265 districts received "financial warning" (2022-23)
- 43 districts achieved perfect 4.0 score

**Kent's Position:**
Kent's specific score was not publicly available in search results, but with a 12.7% fund balance ratio and positive fund balance, Kent would likely score above the warning threshold. However, the trend trajectory (declining enrollment, structural deficit, fund balance draws) puts Kent on a concerning trajectory.

## KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY

### Finding 1: Repeated Pattern of Enrollment Projection Failures

Kent School District has experienced at least two major enrollment projection failures:
1. **2015-2016:** 500-student shortfall, costing $7M in revenue, fund balance went from $14.1M to negative $6.9M
2. **2025-2026:** 792-student shortfall, costing $8.2M in K-12 revenue alone

Both times, spending reductions were delayed. In 2015, "spending reductions did not begin until the spring of 2016, and that delay complicated the district's financial challenges." In 2025, the budget was adopted in June with flat enrollment assumptions despite years of documented decline.

### Finding 2: Structural Use of One-Time Revenue

The district used $99M in ESSER funds -- approximately half to "keep staff whole despite enrollment decline" -- effectively masking a structural deficit. Including $31.5M of one-time ESSER in the 2023-24 budget while knowing enrollment was declining was a budgetary practice that deferred rather than addressed the underlying problem.

### Finding 3: Board Was Warned But Action Was Delayed

- December 2023: Superintendent acknowledged 1,329 fewer students than projected
- January 2024: Board told of $31.5M shortfall
- May 2025: Finance Director noted district was operating at $12.3M deficit (higher than the anticipated $9.7M)
- June 2025: Board adopted budget with flat enrollment assumption (24,222)
- October 2025: Actual enrollment 23,430 (shortfall of 792)

The gap between acknowledged enrollment decline (December 2023) and the use of flat/optimistic enrollment projections in budgets (June 2025) represents a critical governance failure.

### Finding 4: Legal Liability Framework Exists

RCW 28A.505.150 establishes personal civil liability for directors who "knowingly or negligently" incur expenditures exceeding appropriations. If budgets were adopted based on enrollment projections that leadership knew were unrealistic, this could trigger liability. The standard is "knowingly or negligently" -- not intentionally.

### Finding 5: Washington State Has Escalation Mechanisms

The state has a clear escalation path: Financial Warning -> Binding Conditions -> Financial Oversight Committee -> Enhanced Financial Oversight -> Dissolution. As of 2025, eight districts are under binding conditions or oversight. Kent is NOT among them, likely because its fund balance ($60M) has masked the structural deficit. However, the trajectory (drawing $12.4M from fund balance in 2025-26, projected $35M deficit in 2026-27) could trigger intervention.

### Finding 6: Comparable Districts Acted More Decisively

- Tacoma made $30M in cuts across 2023-24 and 2024-25
- Renton proactively made $8.2M in reductions for 2023-24
- Kent's primary response was drawing from fund balance and relying on ESSER, deferring structural cuts

### Finding 7: Voter Trust Has Eroded

Three consecutive levy/bond failures (April 2023, November 2023, April 2024) before a 55%-reduced measure passed in November 2024 suggests significant public distrust of district financial management. This mirrors the Marysville pattern where levy failures preceded the financial crisis.

### Finding 8: The 2015 Precedent Should Have Changed Behavior

The 2015-2016 enrollment shortfall was nearly identical in nature to the 2025-2026 shortfall. The district acknowledged in 2017 that the delay in responding to the 2015 shortfall "complicated the district's financial challenges by further reducing its fund balance." Yet the same pattern -- overoptimistic enrollment projections followed by delayed spending reductions -- recurred almost exactly.
