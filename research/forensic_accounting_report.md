## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### The Smoking Gun

Kent School District administration identified a "$31.5 million structural deficit" in November 2023 and explicitly named the risk of spending "$30 million/year in non-recurring revenue" as early as June 2022 — yet subsequent budgets projected deficits of only $6.5M-$9.7M, and the June 2025 budget was adopted using flat enrollment assumptions (24,222 students) despite five consecutive years of documented decline. Actual October 2025 enrollment came in at 23,430 — a 792-student shortfall that blew the deficit from $14.9M to $35.3M.

### Financial Impact Summary

| Fiscal Year | Projected Deficit | Actual/Revised Deficit | Miss Factor |
|-------------|------------------:|-----------------------:|:-----------:|
| 2024-25 | -$9.65M | -$12.3M+ | 1.3x |
| 2025-26 | -$6.50M | -$22.84M | 3.5x |
| 2026-27 (proj) | -$7.24M | -$30.5M to -$35M | 4.2x-4.8x |

**Cumulative Impact:**
- $99.95M in one-time ESSER funds exhausted (approximately half used to maintain staffing levels enrollment no longer supported)
- Fund balance projected to reach **negative $89.3M (-15.3%)** without cuts
- Up to 408 provisional teachers facing nonrenewal for 2026-27
- $35M in permanent cuts required over three years
- Three consecutive levy/bond failures (2023-2024), requiring 55% reduction before one passed

## SECTION 1: FINANCIAL ANALYSIS

### 1.1 Enrollment Projection Accuracy

Kent School District has systematically over-projected enrollment:

| School Year | Projected AAFTE | Actual AAFTE | Variance | % Error |
|-------------|----------------:|-------------:|---------:|--------:|
| 2015-16 | 27,643 | 27,089 | -554 | -2.0% |
| 2020-21 | 26,418 | 25,323 | -1,095 | -4.1% |
| 2021-22 | 25,776 | ~24,690 | ~-1,086 | -4.2% |
| 2022-23 | 23,781 | 24,090 | +309 | +1.3% |
| 2023-24 | 24,192 | 23,938 | -254 | -1.0% |
| 2024-25 | 23,945 | 23,902 | -43 | -0.2% |
| **2025-26** | **24,222** | **23,430** | **-792** | **-3.3%** |

**Finding:** In 7 of 8 measured years, enrollment was over-projected. The district's own staff acknowledged: "there were a couple years where we really over-budgeted our enrollment, and that's dangerous." Despite this acknowledged pattern, the June 2025 budget assumed flat enrollment — **higher than the prior year's actual**.

### 1.2 Revenue Impact

At approximately $10,354 per K-12 student in state revenue:
- 792-student shortfall (2025-26): **$8.2M** direct revenue loss
- 413-student Inclusive Education decline: **$6.2M** additional loss
- Federal funding reductions: **$6.0M**
- **Total additional gap: $20.4M** on top of the $14.9M already projected

### 1.3 ESSER Dependency Analysis

| ESSER Metric | Amount |
|--------------|-------:|
| Total ESSER received | $99.95M |
| General Fund portion | $94.86M |
| Annual spend rate (2020-2024) | ~$30M/year |
| Purpose: maintaining staff despite enrollment decline | ~$50M (est.) |
| Purpose: COVID operations (PPE, health, safety) | ~$50M (est.) |

**Finding:** The district explicitly acknowledged in June 2022 that it was spending "$30 million/year in non-recurring revenue" and asked "How does this Revenue Get Replaced?" The answer, evidently, was: it doesn't. Instead of planning a phased wind-down aligned to actual enrollment, the district drew down ESSER funds while deferring structural adjustments.

### 1.4 Fund Balance Trajectory

| Period | Fund Balance | Context |
|--------|-------------:|---------|
| 2015-16 start | $14.1M | Before prior enrollment shortfall |
| 2017-18 start | -$6.9M | After 2015 shortfall + delayed response |
| Jan 2021 | $58.4M | ESSER-inflated peak |
| Jan 2022 | $41.7M | Down $16.7M in one year |
| FY2023-24 end | $69.79M | **Board told "around $46M" at adoption** |
| FY2024-25 (proj) | ~$60.1M | 9.9% of expenditures |
| FY2028-29 (proj) | **-$89.3M** | Without $35M+ in cuts |

**Critical Discrepancy:** At the August 2024 budget adoption, Budget Director Tylor stated the ending fund balance would be "around $46 million." The actual FY2023-24 ending balance was $69.79M — approximately **$18.8M more than disclosed**. The source and reason for this variance were not explained to the board.

### 1.5 Expenditure Growth vs. Revenue Growth

- Revenue growth: **5.7%/year** (~$25M)
- Expenditure growth: **7.4%/year** (~$31M)
- Annual widening gap: **~$8.5M/year**
- Special education: systematically over budget by $9.6M-$12.79M during FY2024-25

## SECTION 2: GOVERNANCE ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Board Oversight Mechanisms — Adopted but Not Enforced

| Mechanism | Status | Finding |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Fiscal Recovery Task Force (Oct 2018) | **Disappeared** | No evidence of reports or activity after March 2019 amendment |
| Policy 6022 Fund Balance (5% minimum) | Active but inadequate | 5% = ~2.5 weeks of operations; increase to 10% not recommended until March 2025 |
| Monthly financial reports (WAC 392-123-115) | Active | Required "written explanation of significant deviations" — quality of explanations unknown |
| Superintendent evaluation rubric (Oct 2022) | Framework exists | Requires "immediately notifies board regarding factors that may make budget unsustainable" — deficit tripled |
| Finance/Audit Committee (Mar 2018) | Established | No evidence of independent financial oversight findings |

### 2.2 The Fiscal Recovery Task Force — A Critical Governance Gap

In October 2018, during a previous enrollment-driven budget crisis (700-student shortfall, $7.97M revenue loss), the board established a Fiscal Recovery Task Force with:
- Monthly monitoring requirements
- Financial dashboards
- Board co-presentations

**The Task Force produced an action plan in October 2018, amended it in March 2019, and then vanished from the documentary record.** If this oversight mechanism had remained active, the current crisis might have been identified and addressed years earlier.

### 2.3 Board Information Quality

**What the board asked for but may not have received:**
- Board President Margell (June 2024): "that's just a piece that's missing" regarding allocation information; board needs "more context to base that information off of instead of just assuming we don't have the money"
- Director Song (January 2024): "we don't have that total figure, we don't know how many positions are being cut"
- Director Cook (January 2024): Voted against cuts authorization 4-1, calling for community involvement and specificity

**What the public perceived:**
- 2023 bond survey: some community members saw a "budget surplus" even as the district faced a $31.5M structural deficit
- Board member (June 2024): "There's a lot of concern and trust in the district, warranted or not, that is what I still to this day believe is causing us to fail these levies"

## SECTION 3: LEGAL AND REGULATORY ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Potential Statutory Violations

**RCW 28A.505.150 — Director Personal Liability:**
> Directors, officers, or employees who **knowingly or negligently** violate budget appropriation limits are held "civilly liable, jointly and severally, for such expenditures in excess of such appropriation(s), including consequential damages." If the violation is **knowing**, the director "shall immediately forfeit his or her office or employment."

**Application:** If budgets were adopted based on enrollment projections administration knew or should have known were overstated — particularly after the June 2022 identification of the structural deficit and December 2023 acknowledgment of 1,329 fewer students — this could constitute negligent approval of budgets with inflated revenue assumptions.

**WAC 392-123-115 — Monthly Budget Status Reports:**
> Districts must provide "a brief written explanation of any significant deviations in revenue and/or expenditure projections that may affect the financial status of the district."

**Application:** If monthly reports did not adequately explain the significance of enrollment shortfalls relative to budget assumptions, this constitutes a regulatory violation.

**RCW 28A.505.130 — Balanced Budget Requirement:**
> Budgets exceeding available resources are "null and void and shall not be considered an appropriation."

**Application:** Budgets based on systematically overstated enrollment inherently overstate revenue. If the district's budgeted revenues were predicated on enrollment levels known to be unrealistic, the budget may have been technically invalid.

### 3.2 OSPI Escalation Risk

Kent is not currently under binding conditions. However:
- Eight Washington districts are currently under binding conditions or financial oversight (Bellevue, Marysville, Tukwila, Yelm, Shelton, Mabton, Mount Baker, Prescott)
- Kent's trajectory matches pre-binding-conditions patterns: structural deficit, declining enrollment, fund balance draws, deferred cuts
- If fund balance drops below the ability to produce a balanced budget, Kent would be required to petition OSPI for binding conditions (WAC 392-123-060)
- **Projected timeline to crisis:** Without $35M+ in cuts, fund balance reaches negative territory by ~2027-28

### 3.3 Historical Precedent — Kent's Own 2015 Failure

| Factor | 2015-16 Crisis | 2025-26 Crisis |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Enrollment shortfall | 500 students | 792 students |
| Revenue loss | ~$7M | $8.2M (K-12 alone) |
| Fund balance impact | $14.1M to -$6.9M | $60.1M to projected -$89.3M |
| Response delay | Cuts didn't start until spring 2016 | Structural deficit identified June 2022; major cuts not until 2026-27 |
| Lesson learned? | Board acknowledged delay "complicated challenges" | **Same pattern repeated** |

### 3.4 Comparative District Response

| District | Enrollment Change | Budget Response | Timing |
|----------|:-----------------:|----------------|--------|
| **Kent** | -8.7% (10yr) | ESSER + fund balance draws; $35M cuts deferred to 2026-27 | **3-4 years after identification** |
| Tacoma | -4.6% | $30M cuts across 2023-24 and 2024-25 | Within 1-2 years |
| Renton | -8.3% | $8.2M reductions 2023-24; $11M+ 2024-25 | Proactive |
| Bellevue | Declining | Entered binding conditions July 2025 | After reserves depleted |
| Seattle | -280 students | $105M deficit; school closures considered | Delayed |

**Kent's response was among the most delayed of comparable districts**, relying more heavily on ESSER and fund balance drawdowns than peer districts.

## SECTION 4: HYPOTHESIS TESTING

### Hypothesis A: Incompetence (Honest Projection Errors)
**Evidence for:** Enrollment projection is inherently uncertain; pandemic disrupted all forecasting models; the 2022-23 projection was actually too low (+309). State-wide enrollment decline affected many districts.

**Evidence against:** The district had a professional consulting firm (MGT), demographic studies (Les Kendrick), and access to King County birth data. After 5 consecutive years of decline, projecting flat enrollment for 2025-26 is not a reasonable error — it is a choice. The district had prior experience with this exact failure in 2015.

**Verdict:** Incompetence alone cannot explain using flat enrollment assumptions in June 2025 after five years of continuous decline and explicit acknowledgment of the pattern.

### Hypothesis B: Deliberate Deception (Knowing Overstatement)
**Evidence for:** The $18.8M fund balance discrepancy at adoption; using flat enrollment after years of decline; the gap between internal acknowledgment ($31.5M structural deficit) and external projections ($6.5M-$9.7M deficits in budgets); maintaining ESSER-funded staffing without off-ramp planning.

**Evidence against:** Administration did present the structural deficit at board meetings; multi-year forecasts did show deficits; the superintendent's evaluation rubric was adopted with financial accountability requirements. The information was, in broad strokes, available.

**Verdict:** The evidence does not conclusively prove deliberate deception, but the pattern of minimizing deficits in budget assumptions while acknowledging larger structural problems in separate presentations is deeply concerning.

### Hypothesis C: Institutional Optimism Bias + Structural Incentives
**Evidence for:** Over-projecting enrollment delays politically painful cuts. Drawing from fund balance avoids immediate staffing reductions. Presenting a $6.5M deficit is more palatable than a $31.5M one. The state funding true-up mechanism creates a short-term incentive to overproject. Board members asking for information "that's just a piece that's missing" suggests information asymmetry, not complicity.

**Verdict: Most supported by evidence.** The pattern is consistent with institutional optimism bias reinforced by structural incentives to defer pain. Administration had the data showing structural problems but consistently used more favorable assumptions in budget documents. The board was warned in general terms but not given specific enough information to challenge assumptions effectively.

## SECTION 6: RED FLAGS REQUIRING FURTHER INVESTIGATION

### Tier 1 — Highest Priority

1. **The $18.8M Fund Balance Discrepancy.** Board told "around $46M"; actual was $69.79M. This is either incompetent accounting or deliberate understatement. Both are serious.

2. **Flat Enrollment Projection After 5 Years of Decline.** June 2025 budget assumed 24,222 AAFTE — higher than 2024-25 actual of 23,902. No reasonable methodology produces this output given the trend. Requires examination of internal communications about who set the number and why.

3. **$31.5M Structural Deficit Shrinking to $6.5M in Budgets.** The November 2023 presentation showed a $31.5M structural deficit. Subsequent budget projections showed deficits of $9.65M and $6.5M. Either the $31.5M was overstated for effect, or the budgets systematically understated reality. Both are governance failures.

### Tier 2 — Important

4. **Fiscal Recovery Task Force Disappearance.** Established with specific oversight functions; no evidence of activity after March 2019. Did someone actively terminate it, or did it simply stop meeting?

5. **Zero Public Comments at $570M Budget Hearing.** Was the hearing adequately publicized per RCW 28A.505.050 (two notices over consecutive weeks)? This may indicate inadequate public notice rather than public apathy.

6. **ESSER Spending on Recurring Commitments.** If $50M+ in one-time ESSER funds were used to maintain staff positions that enrollment didn't support, this is a textbook misuse of one-time revenue — one that any competent financial manager would flag.

### Tier 3 — Contextual

7. **Clean Audit During Crisis.** The FY2023 SAO audit found no issues while the structural deficit was $31.5M. This suggests the audit scope did not include budget assumption reasonableness — a scope limitation that should be expanded.

8. **Special Education Over-Budget Pattern.** $9.6M-$12.79M over budget is not a rounding error. Either the budgeting is deliberately understating costs or the service delivery model is unsustainable.

9. **Classified Staff Disproportionate Cuts.** Classified salaries cut 1.53% vs certificated 0.34%. The rationale ("more classified vacant positions") deserves scrutiny.

## CONCLUSION

This investigation finds a pattern of **institutional optimism bias reinforced by structural incentives to defer politically painful decisions**. The evidence shows:

1. **Administration knew.** The structural deficit was explicitly identified in June 2022, quantified at $31.5M in November 2023, and acknowledged by the superintendent as early as January 2024.

2. **Budgets didn't reflect that knowledge.** Deficit projections in adopted budgets were 2x-5x smaller than the identified structural deficit. Enrollment projections were flat or optimistic despite a documented multi-year decline.

3. **The board was given mixed signals.** Work session presentations showed the structural problem; budget adoption documents used rosier assumptions. Board members who asked for more information were told it was coming.

4. **Oversight mechanisms were established then abandoned.** The Fiscal Recovery Task Force, designed for exactly this scenario, disappeared. Fund balance policy was set too low. The superintendent evaluation rubric existed but its application is unknown.

5. **This exact failure happened before.** The 2015-2016 enrollment shortfall was nearly identical. The district acknowledged the delayed response "complicated challenges." Yet the same pattern recurred.

The question for the board and community is not whether mistakes were made — the evidence on that point is overwhelming. The question is whether the pattern of minimizing deficits in budget documents while acknowledging larger structural problems in separate presentations constitutes negligence under RCW 28A.505.150, and what accountability mechanism will ensure it does not happen again.
