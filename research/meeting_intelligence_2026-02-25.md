## CLAIM #3: "The decline in enrollment is driven by the King County birth rate, which is outside our control"

**Why it will be made:** The Feb 4, 2026 Budget Update document leading tonight's presentation opens with explicit birth rate data. Staff routinely frame enrollment decline as a demographic inevitability to deflect scrutiny of district-controlled factors.

**What they'll likely say:** *"The King County birth rate has dropped 13.8% since 2016, and kindergarten enrollment has dropped 8.3% since 2021-22. This is a regional demographic trend that is driving our enrollment decline and is largely outside of our control."*

**Documentary Response:**

The birth rate narrative is **partially accurate but incomplete** in important ways:

**What the documents confirm:**
- King County birth rate declined **13.8% since 2016** (Budget Update, Enrollment slide)
- Kindergarten enrollment dropped **8.3% since 2021-22** (1,874 → 1,684 per Budget Update chart — though the Dec 2025 table shows 2025-26 kindergarten at **1,693**)

**What the documents reveal that complicates this narrative:**

1. **The enrollment decline is outpacing birth rate trends.** Total enrollment fell from 25,455 in 2019-20 to 23,430 in October 2025 — a decline of **2,025 students or 8.0% in 6 years**, while the birth rate drop of 13.8% spans a longer horizon and would not yet be fully felt at the high school level.

2. **Staff levels did not track enrollment.** Lisa Tylor stated on **2024-07-10**: *"Over the past 5 years, enrollment has declined in excess of 8%, whereas our staffing levels have gone up almost to about the same percentage."* This directly contradicts the claim that the problem is purely demographic.

3. **The 2025-26 projection was missed by 792 students (3.3%) in just the first October count.** The district projected 24,222 for 2025-26 at adoption; actual October enrollment was 23,430. This is a significant projection error that cannot be attributed to birth rate data alone — birth rate data for kindergartners born in 2020 was available at the time of projection.

4. **Inclusive Education enrollment declined by 413 students** — this category does not directly track birth rates and suggests additional factors at play.

5. **The projected trajectory continues declining:** Per the Budget Update enrollment table, projections show further declines to **23,192 (2026-27), 22,857 (2027-28), and 22,508 (2028-29)** — a loss of another 922 students from current levels over three years.

**Source:** Feb 4, 2026 Budget Update (Enrollment slides, birth rate chart, kindergarten enrollment chart, enrollment projection table); Financial Statement December 2025, Introduction; Past meeting transcript 2024-07-10 (Tylor quote on staffing)

## CLAIM #4: "The state underfunds our special education program by approximately $10 million or more, and this is a major driver of our deficit"

**Why it will be made:** Special education underfunding has been cited in every budget cycle. Parungao stated on **2025-06-25** that "our special education program is overspending by about $10 million to date." The Budget Update document shows dramatically increased special education state revenues, making this a likely updated claim tonight.

**What they'll likely say:** *"The state provides approximately $57-58 million for special education, but our actual costs to serve our students with disabilities exceed $80-90 million. This structural gap of $20-30 million must be covered by our general fund levy dollars — and the state has been chronically underfunding this for years."*

**Documentary Response:**

The records confirm a **real and significant** special education funding gap, but the figures have changed substantially:

**Historical record of the gap:**

| Date | State SpEd Revenue | Local Subsidy Needed | Source |
|------|-------------------|---------------------|--------|
| Jul 2024 | $68M received, $91M budgeted | **$21-23M local** | Tylor 2024-07-10 |
| Jun 2025 | "overspending by ~$10M to date" | Parungao 2025-06-25 |
| 2024-25 Actual | **$57.3M** state special ed revenue | F196 data in Budget Update |
| 2025-26 Budget | Not yet shown in available docs | Budget Update (incomplete data provided) |

**Key facts from the Budget Update documents:**

- Special Education state revenue grew from **$33.5M (2020-21) to $57.3M (2024-25)** — a **71% increase** over 5 years, or +10.7% annually (State Special Purpose table in Budget Update)
- Despite this growth, the local subsidy requirement has not disappeared because **actual spending on inclusive education also grew**
- The **Dec 2025 Financial Statement** reveals a new factor: **Inclusive Education enrollment declined by 413 students**, which *reduced* state special education funding by **$6.2 million** in 2025-26 — meaning the gap the district anticipated may now be different than projected, with less state revenue *and* potentially lower costs

**The $131 million underfunding claim:** Parungao stated on **2025-06-25** that the district is underfunded by **$131 million overall** by the state across multiple categories (overformula, MSOC, highly capable, multilingual education, transportation), with the community providing **$80.4 million in EP&O levy** to bridge part of that gap. This aggregate framing obscures which specific categories are most underfunded.

**Challenge question:** The district should be asked to reconcile why special education state revenues increased 71% over 5 years yet the structural deficit persists, and how the 413-student decline in Inclusive Education enrollment affects both revenues and costs for 2025-26.

**Source:** Feb 4, 2026 Budget Update (State Special Purpose revenue table); Financial Statement December 2025 (Introduction, p.1); Past meeting transcripts 2024-07-10 (Tylor), 2025-06-25 (Parungao)

## CLAIM #5: "The federal funding uncertainty — potential cuts to Title I, Title II, Title III, and Title IV — represents an additional risk of up to $17 million to our budget"

**Why it will be made:** Parungao raised federal funding threats explicitly in June 2025 and the political environment in early 2026 makes this an even more pressing concern tonight. The district has a pattern of presenting federal risk as a separate line item of alarm.

**What they'll likely say:** *"In addition to our structural deficit, we face significant uncertainty from the federal government. Title I alone represents up to $17 million. Title II gives us over $2 million, Title III nearly $900,000, and Title IV approximately $800,000. Combined, we could lose $3.5 million to $17 million in federal funds that we currently rely on to serve our highest-need students."*

**Documentary Response:**

**What Parungao stated on June 25, 2025** (the most recent on-record figures):

| Federal Program | Amount Cited |
|----------------|-------------|
| Title I (full exposure) | Up to **$17 million** |
| Title II | Over **$2 million/year** |
| Title III | About **$900,000/year** |
| Title IV | Almost **$800,000/year** |
| **Subtotal (II+III+IV)** | **~$3.5 million** |
| **Total maximum exposure** | **~$17 million** (if Title I fully cut) |

**What the Budget Update document confirms for 2024-25 actual federal revenues:**

Per the General Fund Revenue pie chart (OSPI F196 2024-25 data):
- **Federal Special Purpose: $34.1 million total (6% of revenues)**

This $34.1M total means the $17M Title I figure represents approximately **50% of all federal special purpose revenues** — making a full Title I cut an existential threat to the federal funding stream.

**Important documentary distinctions the district may blur:**

1. **The $17 million figure cited in June 2025 was described as a *threat*, not a confirmed cut.** Parungao's exact words were: *"The federal government is threatening us to be able to cut about $3.5 million to about $17 million, which is the entirety of our Title IX funds."* — **Note: Parungao said "Title IX" in the June 2025 transcript, which appears to be a misstatement; the context indicates Title I.** This should be clarified tonight.

2. **The $34.1M in federal revenues in 2024-25** (per Budget Update) is larger than the $17M+$3.5M figures cited, meaning there are additional federal streams (Nutrition Services, IDEA/Special Education federal, etc.) that are separately at risk and have not been fully quantified in public presentations.

3. **The district's December 2025 Financial Statement does not mention any realized federal funding cuts** as of the four-month period ended December 31, 2025, suggesting that as of that date, the threat had not materialized into actual reductions. Tonight's presentation may provide an update on whether any cuts have been confirmed.

**Challenge question:** The board should ask for a complete inventory of all federal funding streams, their federal program designations, their current status, and any official communications received from the U.S. Department of Education regarding potential reductions — distinguishing between *threatened* cuts and *confirmed* reductions.

**Source:** Past meeting transcript 2025-06-25 (Parungao, federal funding quotes); Feb 4, 2026 Budget Update (General Fund Revenue pie chart, F196 2024-25, Federal Special Purpose $34.1M); Financial Statement December 2025 (Introduction — notably silent on federal cuts)
